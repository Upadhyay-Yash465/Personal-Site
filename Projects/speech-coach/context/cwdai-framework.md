# CWDAI Framework — Argument Logic & Structure

The CWDAI framework is a tool for analyzing whether a speech has a complete, logical, and persuasive line of reasoning. Every strong argument has all five elements working together. A speech can be well-delivered and still fail to persuade if any element is missing or weak.

---

## The Line of Reasoning

The core test: **can you draw a single line through the entire speech from Claim → Warrant → Data → Analysis → Impact?**

If that line breaks anywhere — if the data doesn't connect to the warrant, or the impact lands on money instead of people — the argument is structurally incomplete regardless of delivery quality. The goal of CWDAI analysis is to find exactly where that line breaks.

---

## Claim

**What it is:** The main point the speaker is trying to make. The thesis. The position.

A claim is a statement that is arguable — something a reasonable person could disagree with. "The sky is blue" is not a claim. "We should implement universal basic income" is a claim.

**What to look for:**
- Is the claim stated explicitly, or does the listener have to infer it?
- Is it stated early enough (within the first 20% of the speech) that the audience knows what to listen for?
- Is it specific enough to be arguable, or vague enough to be unassailable (and therefore meaningless)?

**Common failures:**
- Claim is never stated — the speaker assumes the audience will figure it out
- Claim is buried at the end (the audience spent the whole speech without direction)
- Claim is so hedged it doesn't take a real position ("we should consider possibly exploring...")

---

## Warrant

**What it is:** The logical bridge between the claim and the data. The *why* — why does the evidence prove the claim?

The warrant is often the most invisible element of an argument. Speakers frequently jump straight from data to claim and assume the connection is obvious. It rarely is.

**Example:**
- Claim: "We should ban single-use plastics"
- Data: "8 million tons of plastic enter the ocean every year"
- Warrant (often missing): "Because ocean plastic is primarily sourced from single-use consumer products, banning them would directly reduce this input"

Without the warrant, the data and claim feel disconnected — the audience is left to do the logical work themselves, and they often don't.

**What to look for:**
- Is the warrant ever stated, or just implied?
- Does the warrant actually hold up logically? Could someone disagree with it?
- Is the warrant a truism ("bad things are bad") or does it do real argumentative work?

**Common failures:**
- No warrant at all — data is cited and claim is stated with nothing connecting them
- Circular warrant — "we should do X because X is good"
- Weak warrant — the connection between data and claim is contestable and the speaker never defends it

---

## Data

**What it is:** Evidence. Any kind — statistics, studies, expert testimony, historical precedent, analogies, personal experience, logical proof.

**The key test: does this evidence ACTUALLY prove the point?**

This is where most speakers fail. They find a statistic that feels relevant and cite it, without asking whether it genuinely supports their warrant and claim. The analysis tool should evaluate not just whether data is present, but whether it is *the right data* and whether it *actually does what the speaker needs it to do.*

**Data quality tiers:**
| Tier | What it looks like |
|------|-------------------|
| Strong | Directly measures the thing being claimed. Peer-reviewed, recent, from a credible source. |
| Adequate | Indirectly supports the claim. Relevant but not airtight. |
| Weak | Tangentially related. Could support many different claims equally. |
| Missing | No evidence offered. The claim is asserted, not argued. |

**What to look for:**
- Is the source credible? Is it recent?
- Does the data measure exactly what the claim is about, or something adjacent?
- Could the same data support the opposite conclusion?
- If the data is a personal story or anecdote, is it representative, or an outlier?

**When to recommend stronger evidence:**
If the data is weak or missing, the analysis should name *what kind of evidence would actually work* — not just say "find better evidence." For example: "a randomized controlled study showing X" or "longitudinal data on Y" or "expert testimony from a practitioner in Z field."

---

## Analysis

**What it is:** The explanation surrounding the claim, warrant, data, and impact. The connective tissue. The words that show the audience *how to think about* the evidence.

Analysis is not the evidence itself — it's what the speaker does with the evidence. It answers: "So what does this mean? Here's why this matters. Here's how this connects to what I just said."

A speech that is all data and no analysis feels like a bibliography. A speech that is all analysis and no data feels like opinion. The ratio matters, and the transitions between elements matter even more.

**What to look for:**
- Does the speaker explain what the data means, or just cite it and move on?
- Is there a clear "therefore" moment — where the speaker explicitly connects evidence to claim?
- Does the analysis stay on point, or does it wander into adjacent topics?
- Is the analysis proportional to the data — i.e., does the speaker spend time explaining strong evidence rather than glossing over it?

**Common failures:**
- "And that's why..." with no actual explanation of how the evidence proves the point
- Over-analysis of weak data, under-analysis of strong data
- Analysis that contradicts the data (speaker misinterprets their own evidence)

---

## Impact

**What it is:** Why the listener should care. The stakes. The consequence of the claim being true or false.

Impact is the moment the speaker answers "so what?" If the claim is right, what happens? If it's wrong? Who is affected, how, and how much?

### The Three Types of Impact

**1. Quantification**
Giving numbers. Scale, magnitude, frequency.
- "3.7 million children die from preventable diseases every year"
- "This policy would cost $400 billion over a decade"
- Good for establishing scale. Weak on its own because numbers are abstract.

**2. Characterization**
Associating the numbers with people. Describing what those people look like, who they are, what their lives are like.
- "3.7 million children — that's the entire population of Los Angeles, wiped out every single year. These are kids who went to school in the morning. Kids with names."
- Characterization takes quantification and makes it human. The numbers become faces.

**3. Humanization**
Zooming into ONE person or one specific example and talking about them in detail.
- "Maria is six years old. She lives in Lagos. Last March, she got malaria. There was a treatment available at the clinic 4 kilometers away. Her family couldn't afford the bus fare."
- Humanization is the most emotionally powerful form of impact because the human brain connects to individuals more strongly than to statistics. One person's story moves an audience more than 3.7 million deaths.

### The Impact Rule: All Good Impacts End With People

Money, policy, systems — these are acceptable intermediate impacts, but they are not final impacts. A final impact always connects back to human beings.

- "This would cost $400 billion" → weak impact. Money is abstract.
- "This would cost $400 billion — that's $1,200 from every American family's paycheck, every year" → better. Now it's people's money.
- "That $1,200 is the difference between a working-class parent making rent in December or not" → strong. Now it's a person's life.

The test: can you replace the word "people" with "statistics" in your impact and still feel something? If yes, your impact isn't landing.

### Impact Hierarchy
Humanization > Characterization > Quantification alone

Most speeches do quantification. Good speeches layer characterization on top. The best speeches zoom into one human being.

---

## How the Elements Work Together

```
Claim:    We should do X
Warrant:  Because X causes Y
Data:     Evidence that X does cause Y
Analysis: Here's what that evidence means and how it connects
Impact:   And here's why Y matters to the people in this room
```

The line of reasoning is the invisible thread. Pull on any element and the others should follow. If the data doesn't connect back to the warrant, the thread is broken. If the impact lands on a system instead of a person, the thread frays at the end.

A speech can have excellent delivery, strong data, and a clear claim — and still fail to persuade if the impact doesn't land on people.
